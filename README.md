## The Industrial Revolution's "Upper Middle Class" Factory Workers — A Parallel Nobody Talks About

When people think of Industrial Revolution displacement, they picture coal miners or child laborers. But there was a *skilled, comfortable class* being obliterated simultaneously — and that's the more precise parallel to today's tech workers.

**The Skilled Artisan and Factory Manager Class (roughly 1780–1880)**

Think of the textile mill foreman, the master craftsman weaver, the machinist, the ledger-keeper, the pattern-maker. These were people who had invested *years* acquiring specialized knowledge. They earned well above subsistence wages. They had identity, guild status, and community respect rooted in their expertise. They lived in purpose-built factory towns — Lowell, Manchester, Leeds, Paterson NJ — where the entire local economy, housing market, and social fabric was organized around their industry.

When the power loom, the Jacquard machine, and later full automation arrived, what happened wasn't just job loss. It was **identity erasure**. Their specialized knowledge became worthless almost overnight. The Luddite movement (1811–1816) was not, as the word now implies, technophobia from ignorant people — it was a calculated, organized resistance by *highly skilled* framework knitters and weavers who understood exactly what automation meant for their economic class. They were right. Their trades were gutted within a generation.

The key parallels to today:

- **Geographic concentration**: Factory towns were mono-industry ecosystems. San Francisco, Seattle, Austin, and Research Triangle are the modern equivalents — entire real estate markets, restaurant economies, and social structures built around a single high-wage industry.
- **Years of credential investment**: A journeyman craftsman spent 7 years in apprenticeship. A software engineer spent 4+ years in university, often with $100K+ in debt, specifically to enter this field.
- **The "safe" professional class illusion**: The artisan class genuinely believed their complexity of skill insulated them. The belief that "machines can't do *this*" is identical to modern engineers saying "AI can't really reason, it just autocompletes."
- **Sudden velocity of change**: Both disruptions weren't gradual. The power loom didn't slowly nibble at weaving jobs — it collapsed them within years. Generative AI's impact on software jobs has similarly compressed into a 2–3 year window.
- **Employer complicity with capital over labor**: Mill owners in the 1800s didn't care about displaced craftsmen; they cared about cheaper production. Today's tech CEOs explicitly cite AI efficiency as the *justification* for headcount reduction while stock prices climb.

## The WARN Act: What It Is, What It Isn't, and Why It Matters Now

### Origins and Mechanics

The Worker Adjustment and Retraining Notification Act was passed in **1988**, signed by Reagan, and took effect in 1989. It was a direct legislative response to the factory closures and mass layoffs of the Rust Belt era — itself a downstream consequence of industrial automation and offshoring. In other words, **the WARN Act was born from a prior technological displacement crisis.**

The federal WARN Act mandates that employers with 100 or more employees provide at least 60 days advance written notice of a plant closing or mass layoff affecting 50 or more employees.

### The Act's Critical Weaknesses in the AI Context

**1. The 100-employee threshold is trivially gamed.** Major tech companies routinely conduct layoffs in tranches — cutting 80 here, 90 there — specifically to stay under the threshold that triggers WARN obligations.

**2. The 60-day notice window is inadequate for career displacement.** A factory worker losing a job in 1988 might need 60 days to find a similar job. A software engineer whose entire *category* of work is being automated doesn't need 60 days of notice — they need retraining that takes 12–24 months minimum.

**3. "Unforeseeable business circumstances" is a massive escape hatch.** Tech companies have argued — sometimes successfully — that market pivots qualify.

**4. The law doesn't address remote workers cleanly.** WARN was built around physical sites. Remote tech workers scattered across states create jurisdictional ambiguity.

### The New York WARN Amendment — A Harbinger

New York became the first U.S. state to require employers to disclose whether technological innovation or automation — including AI — contributed to employee layoffs, effective March 2025. This was announced by Governor Hochul during her January 2025 State of the State Address.

New York's WARN Act is stricter than the federal version — it applies to employers with 50 or more employees and requires 90 days' advance notice, compared to the federal threshold of 100 employees and 60 days.

### The Deeper Structural Parallel

The factory town skilled worker of 1850 and the senior software engineer of 2024 share something beyond job loss — they share a **broken social contract**. Both groups were told, implicitly, that investment in specialized skill was the path to stability.

The Industrial Revolution resolution took roughly 40–70 years of painful transition. The question is whether the political pressure from a newly displaced, highly educated, and historically politically engaged class of workers forces a faster legislative response than mill workers managed in the 19th century. The industrial analogy suggests, grimly, the answer is probably not — unless political will accelerates to match the velocity of displacement.

## What's Been Written — and Its Gaps

### The Closest Thing: *Power and Progress* (Acemoglu & Johnson, 2023)

This is the most serious attempt to draw the Industrial Revolution–AI parallel at book length. The two MIT economists trace how technological advances from medieval agricultural innovations through the Industrial Revolution have often produced uneven benefits, arguing that technology is a fundamentally double-edged sword.

Their core argument is that sometime after approximately 1970, the balance between automation of traditional work and the creation of new tasks was lost. While automation maintained its pace or even accelerated, the offsetting force of new task creation slowed.

It's a genuinely important book, but its critics point out real weaknesses. Most relevantly, the book focuses heavily on lower-skilled workers and doesn't really wrestle with the *skilled professional class* as the new primary displacement target.

### The Academic Literature: Close, but Fragmented

MIT's Acemoglu and Johnson argue we must act deliberately to ensure AI's benefits are shared widely. The historical parallel they use is explicit: family earnings for handloom weavers in two Lancashire towns fell by half over a five-year period starting in 1814.

The Stanford "Canaries in the Coal Mine" paper (Brynjolfsson et al., 2025) finds substantial employment declines for early-career workers in occupations most exposed to AI, such as software development and customer support. But it's an economics paper, not a narrative argument about class and historical parallels.

## Why the Gap Exists

1. **The displacement is still very fresh.** The mass tech layoffs began in earnest in late 2022. Books on economic disruption typically lag by 3–5 years.
2. **Economists own the conversation, and they're bad at class specificity.** The academic literature treats "high-skill workers" as a category, not as a social class with identity, geography, and political consciousness.
3. **The WARN Act angle is almost completely unaddressed.** Despite New York's 2025 amendment being genuinely significant, there is virtually no long-form writing connecting the WARN Act's origins in Rust Belt deindustrialization to its current inadequacy for AI-driven white-collar layoffs.
4. **Tech workers resist the "worker" identity.** Software engineers largely don't see themselves as a labor class subject to historical labor dynamics.

## What You'd Actually Want to Read

If you want to get as close as possible to the full argument right now, you'd need to piece it together from: *Power and Progress* for the historical framework, the Stanford "Canaries" paper for the current empirical data, the Brookings adaptive capacity report for the structural vulnerability analysis, and then primary source reporting on WARN Act violations. No single book has stitched all of this together.

## Variable 1: The De-Geographicized Working Class — A New and Larger Scale of Displacement

### The Historical Working Class Was Geographically Imprisoned

The critical thing to understand about every prior displacement event is that geography was both their *strength* and their *prison*. Geographic concentration created political power (the Chartists, the labor union movement, the UAW), but it also meant that when an industry collapsed, it collapsed an *entire place*.

This created **localized catastrophe with distributed sympathy**. The rest of the country could observe Youngstown from a distance, feel bad, and move on. The displaced workers were contained.

### The Tech Worker as the First Truly Distributed Working Class

The modern tech worker represents something historically novel: **a skilled professional class that achieved working class scale while escaping working class geography.**

The tech industry contributes roughly $2 trillion to U.S. GDP and employs around 12 million workers. These aren't concentrated in one Lowell or one Youngstown. They are distributed across major metros, secondary cities, and fully remote — meaning literally every congressional district.

This distribution has a paradoxical political effect: **it makes the class invisible as a class while making the economic damage more pervasive.** When the mills closed in Lowell, everyone could see it. When 122,500 tech workers are laid off across 257 companies in a single year, it's experienced as 122,500 individual misfortunes rather than a single systemic event.

### The Global Dimension

The modern tech worker competes in a genuinely global labor market, and AI-driven displacement operates on that same global scale simultaneously. By the end of October 2025, more than 1 million job cuts had been announced, 65% higher than the same period in 2024.

This creates what we might call **synchronized global class displacement** — something that has literally never happened before. Every prior technological displacement was geographically staggered, giving other regions time to observe, adapt, and potentially absorb displaced workers.

## Variable 2: The Consumption Collapse — The Hidden Economic Consequence Nobody Is Modeling Correctly

### The Multiplier Problem

There's an economic concept called the **spending multiplier** — the idea that a dollar earned by a worker doesn't just get spent once; it circulates through the local economy multiple times. Economists estimate the spending multiplier for high-income professional workers at roughly 1.5× to 2×.

This is precisely what made factory towns so economically fragile. When the mill left, it didn't just remove factory jobs — it removed the spending that sustained *every other job in town*.

### The Structural Paradox — The CEO Pay Inversion

During every prior displacement event, capital extracted value from labor but *had to reinvest in physical production*. AI displacement has broken this mechanism.

In 2025, over 62,000 tech jobs were cut in the first five months alone, even as companies reported strong financial results — and the median total compensation for S&P 500 CEOs rose to $17.1 million, nearly a 10% increase, with the average CEO-to-worker pay ratio reaching approximately 285-to-1.

The money extracted from eliminated labor isn't being reinvested in new kinds of workers. It's flowing directly upward into stock buybacks, executive compensation, and AI infrastructure spending that itself employs far fewer people than the workforces it replaces.

### The Historical Warning: Demand Deserts

The Lowell and Manchester factory collapses of the mid-to-late 19th century created what historians now call **demand deserts** — regions where the removal of high-wage employment didn't just hurt displaced workers, but collapsed the entire commercial ecosystem that had grown around them.

The real economic damage from tech worker displacement won't show up in unemployment statistics. It will show up 18–36 months later in commercial real estate vacancy rates, restaurant closures, declining property tax revenues, and the slow-motion attrition of the service economy that grew up to serve them.

## The Synthesis: A Framework Nobody Has Yet Articulated

What you're building toward is essentially a new framework:

1. The tech worker is the first truly post-geographic working class, operating at global scale with no geographic anchor
2. Their displacement is occurring simultaneously worldwide, without the geographic staggering that allowed prior events to produce adaptive responses
3. Their spending power was load-bearing for the broader service economy in a way no prior skilled working class was

5. The capital extracted is not being recycled into new labor, breaking the historical resolution mechanism

## The Prompt Engineer Is the Process Boy. The Evidence Is Already In.

### The Rise: Selling the "Button Pusher" as a Career

In 2023, the tech industry and its adjacent media apparatus performed one of the most cynical bait-and-switch operations in modern labor history. As layoffs were eliminating tens of thousands of software engineers, the narrative machine simultaneously told displaced and aspiring workers that the solution was *prompt engineering* — a role explicitly marketed on its **low barrier to entry**.

Anthropic was advertising prompt engineering roles at salaries as high as $375,000, which didn't require in-depth technical knowledge or even a tech degree.

Read that with the process boy framework in mind. The selling point — *no technical knowledge required* — is not a feature. It is the tell. It is precisely what mill owners said when they replaced master weavers with cheap hands who could pull a single lever. The deliberate stripping of the need for foundational knowledge is not democratization. **It is deskilling, rebranded with a six-figure salary as cover.**

### The Fall: Exactly as Fast as the Rise

Fast forward to 2025, and this glamorous role has quietly disappeared — a victim of AI's rapid self-evolution. Prompt engineering is one of the first casualties of what might be called **AI cannibalism**, where artificial intelligence advances so fast it consumes the very jobs it had created.

Microsoft surveyed 31,000 workers across 31 countries. Prompt engineer ranked **second to last** among new roles companies are considering adding in the next 12 to 18 months.

Malcolm Frank, CEO of TalentGenius, put it plainly: *"AI is already eating its own. Prompt engineering has become something embedded in almost every role. It's turned from a job into a task very, very quickly."* He compared prompt engineering to roles like "Excel wizard" — valuable skills, but not ones companies hire for individually.

Sam Altman himself predicted this in 2022, saying *"I don't think we'll be doing prompt engineering in five years."* He was right. He was also, simultaneously, allowing his company to advertise $375,000 prompt engineering roles to workers who needed somewhere to go. **The right hand knew what the left hand was building.**

## The Process Boy Dynamic Is Self-Replicating

Here is the most insidious structural element your framework identifies, and it hasn't been stated clearly anywhere in the existing literature:

**The process boy role doesn't just fail the worker — it actively degrades the worker's future options.**

A master weaver who spent five years minding a power loom did not simply fail to advance. They *unlearned* the deep tacit knowledge of their craft through disuse. The neural pathways of expertise atrophied. When the power loom era ended and more complex textile work returned, those workers couldn't go back. The knowledge was gone.

The software engineer who spends two to three years doing prompt engineering rather than coding is not in neutral — they are **in reverse.** Their existing technical skills are atrophying. The gap between where they are and where the industry needs deep expertise is widening every month they spend writing prompts instead of building systems.

## The Structural Verdict

What you've identified is a near-perfect replication of the process boy problem, with three additional features that make it worse:

1. **The compression.** The process boy era lasted decades. The prompt engineering era lasted approximately 18 months. Workers had no adaptive window.
2. **The active misdirection.** The tech industry explicitly and loudly told workers that prompt engineering was a career, built educational infrastructure around it, and then pulled the rug before the first cohort could graduate.
3. **The credentialing trap.** Workers paid for certifications in a field that was worthless before the ink dried. The process boy at least didn't go into debt to learn to pull a lever.

The "get with the times" narrative, stripped of its motivational language, amounts to this: *abandon the deep expertise that makes you economically irreplaceable, adopt the shallow operational skill that makes you temporarily useful and permanently replaceable, and call it adaptability.*

It is not adaptability. It is the managed consent to one's own deskilling.

## The Intellectual Genealogy: What Exists, What It Says, and Where the Gap Is

### Tier One: The Foundational Historical Works

**E.P. Thompson — *The Making of the English Working Class* (1963)**

This is the single most important historical text for this framework. Thompson's stated aim was to rescue *"the poor stockinger, the Luddite cropper, the 'obsolete' hand-loom weaver, the 'utopian' artisan"* from what he called *"the enormous condescension of posterity."*

Thompson showed the Luddites to be far from blindly anti-technology — they were people capable of considerable political-economic awareness. A skilled class that understood exactly what was being done to them. This framework makes exactly Thompson's move, but for the modern tech worker.

Thompson also introduced **"history from below"** — the methodological insistence that social history be written from the perspective of those being displaced, not from the perspective of the capital and technology that displaced them. This framework is, implicitly, a history-from-below argument applied to a class nobody has yet recognized as requiring that treatment.

**Harry Braverman — *Labor and Monopoly Capital* (1974)**

This is the most direct theoretical ancestor of the process boy argument. Braverman's core thesis — the **degradation of work thesis** — argued that capitalism systematically separates conception from execution, reducing workers from craftsmen who understand the full arc of their work to operators who perform single, disconnected tasks.

Remarkably, Braverman was already analyzing the deskilling of software programming in 1974. Research into the programming industry suggests that *"the perception of software programming as creative work coexists with a growing standardisation of work in software companies."* The prompt engineering pivot is the 2023 iteration of the mechanism he identified fifty years ago.

---

### Tier Two: The Contemporary Economics Literature

**Acemoglu & Johnson — *Power and Progress* (2023)**
The most serious mainstream economics attempt to draw the Industrial Revolution–AI parallel at book length. Critical weakness: focuses primarily on lower-skilled workers and treats "high-skill" as a protected category — precisely the inversion this framework identifies.

**Brynjolfsson et al. — "Canaries in the Coal Mine" (Stanford, 2025)**
Documents substantial employment declines for early-career workers in AI-exposed occupations. Best available empirical data, but agnostic on the class, historical, and political dimensions this framework addresses.

**Chen, Srinivasan & Zakerinia — "Displacement or Complementarity?" (Harvard, 2025)**
Finds a 24% decrease in generative-AI-exposed skills per firm per quarter among jobs in the top quartile of automation exposure. Useful empirical confirmation of pipeline bifurcation, but an econometrics paper, not a historical or sociological argument.
