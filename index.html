The Process Boy Problem

AI Displacement, the Capitalist Flywheel, and the Destruction of the Knowledge Worker Pipeline

Prompting Claude.ai with my theories and feelings about the current tech worker layoffs relative to the AI boom Transcript March 2026

Abstract

This paper argues that the artificial intelligence-driven displacement of technology workers constitutes the most structurally dangerous episode of skilled-class obsolescence since the Industrial Revolution — and that the dominant cultural response, encouraging displaced workers to embrace prompt engineering as an adaptive career pivot, replicates with near-perfect fidelity the historical error that condemned British industrial workers to a generation of deskilling and diminished economic mobility. Drawing on historical parallels between guild-era craftsmen and the modern software engineer class, the paper develops a seven-pillar theoretical framework addressing: the novelty of a post-geographic working class; the inadequacy of existing labor protections; the load-bearing role of tech worker spending in the consumer economy; the mutually assured nature of consumption collapse for capital and labor alike; the self-defeating short-termism of the prompt engineering pivot; and the catastrophic long-term consequences of breaking the education-to-apprentice-to-expert knowledge transmission pipeline. The paper concludes that without deliberate institutional intervention, the current trajectory leads not to productive creative destruction, but to a demand desert, an expert famine, and the voluntary dismantlement of the cognitive infrastructure upon which the next phase of technological development will depend.

§ ICore Argument Pillars

The theoretical framework advanced in this paper rests on seven interconnected pillars, developed in full in the sections below. Together, they constitute a unified argument about the structural, economic, and civilizational stakes of the current moment in artificial intelligence and labor.

  1. 1 The tech worker constitutes the first truly post-geographic, globally synchronized working class — making their displacement uniquely invisible as a political event and uniquely pervasive as an economic one.
  2. 2 The skilled technology worker is the precise historical analogue of the guild craftsman and master artisan of the Industrial Revolution — sharing the same fatal vulnerability: years of credential investment, professional identity rooted in expertise, and the belief that complexity of skill constitutes a permanent moat.
  3. 3 Existing labor protection infrastructure — primarily the federal WARN Act of 1988 — is architecturally mismatched to AI-driven displacement, designed for visible, site-specific closures rather than distributed, rolling, algorithmically managed workforce elimination.
  4. 4 The spending power of the technology working class is load-bearing for the broader consumer economy in a way no prior skilled class was. Its removal does not trim costs — it destroys the demand base that sustains the entire commercial ecosystem calibrated to that spending.
  5. 5 AI-driven displacement is mutually assured economic destruction. Capital extraction without wage replacement collapses the consumer flywheel that generates the returns capital depends upon — making this a structural threat to the ownership class itself.
  6. 6 The dominant cultural prescription — pivoting displaced workers to prompt engineering — is a precise replication of the Industrial Revolution's "process boy" error: managed deskilling into a narrow, terminal, rapidly automated role, sold as adaptation, delivering obsolescence.
  7. 7 The most catastrophic long-term consequence is the rupture of the education-to-apprenticeship-to-expert pipeline. When this pipeline breaks, expertise does not pause — it dies with the last generation that holds it, arriving precisely when AI systems will most require deep human oversight.

§ IIIntroduction: A Crisis Without a Photograph

Every prior episode of mass technological displacement in the industrial age had a photograph. The shuttered mill in Lowell, Massachusetts. The empty factory floor in Youngstown, Ohio. The padlocked gate of the Detroit assembly plant. These images gave the displacement legibility — a single geographic fact that politicians could visit, journalists could document, and historians could use as shorthand for an era of economic trauma.

The artificial intelligence-driven displacement of the technology working class has no photograph. It arrives as a Slack notification at 9 a.m. on a Tuesday, followed by the revocation of access credentials before the worker can clear their desk. It is distributed across a hundred cities, a dozen countries, and several million individual home offices. The scale is unprecedented — by early 2026, over one million tech workers had been displaced globally across 783 companies at a rate exceeding 700 per day — but the event remains, in the precise political sense of the word, invisible.

This invisibility is not incidental. It is the defining structural feature of the first post-geographic working class displacement in human history, and it is precisely what has allowed the institutional, political, and cultural response to lag so catastrophically behind the reality on the ground.

This paper argues that this invisibility, combined with six additional structural features unique to the current moment, makes the AI displacement of technology workers not merely a repeat of prior industrial transitions, but a qualitatively more dangerous and harder-to-reverse episode of skilled-class destruction — with consequences extending to the consumer economy, the ownership class, and the civilization that will eventually depend on the expertise currently being systematically abandoned.

§ III

§ IIIPillar One: The Post-Geographic Working Class

3.1 Geography as the Anchor of Prior Working Classes

The industrial working classes of the eighteenth and nineteenth centuries were defined as much by their location as their labor. The handloom weavers of Lancashire, the framework knitters of Nottinghamshire, the ironworkers of the Black Country, the steel workers of Pittsburgh, and the autoworkers of Detroit were socioeconomically inseparable from the geographies that produced and concentrated them. This geographic concentration was simultaneously their political strength and their structural vulnerability.

When industries collapsed, they collapsed places. Lowell, Massachusetts took fifty years to recover from the textile contraction. Youngstown, Ohio has not fully recovered from the steel collapse of the 1970s. These are not economic statistics — they are geographic catastrophes, legible on satellite imagery, memorialized in journalism, and politically actionable precisely because they were contained.

3.2 The Technology Worker's Historic Novelty

The modern technology worker represents something that has never existed before in the history of industrial capitalism: a skilled professional class that achieved working-class scale — twelve million workers contributing approximately two trillion dollars to U.S. GDP — while escaping working-class geography entirely.

The damage of their displacement is therefore illegible as a collective event. It arrives as 122,500 individual Slack messages rather than one mill closing, and the political response has been proportionally muted. There is no gate to picket. There is no city council to petition. There is no region whose tax base collapses in a single fiscal quarter and thereby forces a political response.

3.3 Synchronized Global Displacement

The global dimension adds a further layer of structural novelty with no historical precedent. According to Layoffs.fyi, in 2025 alone, 783 layoff events impacted 245,953 tech workers at a rate of 674 people per day — accelerating to 745 per day in early 2026. These cuts are happening simultaneously in the U.S., UK, Germany, India, Canada, and Australia because the companies are multinational and the AI tools are borderless.

This constitutes the first instance in the history of industrial capitalism of synchronized global class displacement — for which no existing institutional, political, or economic framework was designed.

§ IV

§ IVPillar Two: The Master Craftsman Parallel

4.1 The Wrong Historical Analogy

The prevailing popular framing of AI-driven displacement draws its historical analogy from the factory floor: the unskilled or semi-skilled laborer replaced by a machine. This framing is wrong in a consequential way. The unskilled factory laborer who lost work to a power loom lost a job. The master weaver who lost work to a power loom lost a vocation, an identity, a social position, and an irreplaceable accumulation of tacit knowledge that took years to acquire. The damage was categorically different.

4.2 The Luddites Were Right

The precise historical counterpart to the senior software engineer is the guild craftsman: the master weaver, the framework knitter, the machinist, the pattern-maker. Both groups share structural vulnerabilities that, once identified, make the parallel almost uncomfortably exact. As E.P. Thompson demonstrated in The Making of the English Working Class (1963), the Luddites were not technophobes — they were precisely skilled framework knitters who understood exactly what was being done to them. They were right about everything except their ability to stop it.

Harry Braverman's Labor and Monopoly Capital (1974) formalized this as the degradation of work thesis: capitalism systematically separates conception from execution, reducing workers from craftsmen who understand the full arc of their work to operators who perform single, disconnected tasks. Braverman was already analyzing the deskilling of software programming fifty years ago. The prompt engineering pivot is the 2023 iteration of the mechanism he identified in 1974.

NBER historical analysis confirms that the Industrial Revolution produced an initial, sharp decline in the return to skills — specialized human capital was devalued faster than new forms of valuable expertise emerged, and income per capita in affected regions remained depressed for a full generation. The people who lived through the transition did not see the resolution.

§ V

§ VPillar Three: The WARN Act and the Architecture of Inadequate Protection

5.1 Origins and Logic

The Worker Adjustment and Retraining Notification Act (1988) was itself a product of a prior technological displacement crisis — the Rust Belt factory closures of the 1970s and 1980s. Its architecture reflects its origins: it is designed for a factory closing its doors. The federal act requires employers with 100+ employees to provide 60 days advance notice of a mass layoff affecting 50+ workers. New York's state WARN Act is stricter — 50 employees, 90 days notice.

5.2 Five Structural Mismatches

Every structural feature of AI-driven tech displacement falls outside WARN's architecture:

  1. The threshold is trivially gamed. Companies conduct layoffs in tranches — 80 here, 90 there — specifically to remain below WARN obligations. This is documented, not speculated.
  2. The 60-day window addresses displacement, not obsolescence. A factory worker in 1988 needed time to find a similar job. A software engineer whose entire category of work is being automated needs retraining measured in years.
  3. The "unforeseeable circumstances" exemption is a mass escape hatch. Tech companies have successfully argued that market pivots qualify, enabling the "Friday afternoon access revoked" terminations that have become a cultural norm.
  4. Remote workers create jurisdictional chaos. WARN was built around physical sites. Millions of remote workers across state lines produce genuine legal ambiguity.
  5. The law contains no remediation mechanism. Even full compliance delivers notice of termination. It does not address retraining, income replacement, or community stabilization.

5.3 New York's 2025 Amendment

New York became the first U.S. state to require employers to disclose whether AI contributed to layoffs, effective March 2025. Announced by Governor Hochul during her January 2025 State of the State Address, the measure creates data infrastructure for future policy responses. That Tesla and Twitter both faced class action WARN suits while basic compliance is considered exceptional behavior in the industry is itself a precise measure of the law's inadequacy.

§ VI

§ VIPillar Four: The Load-Bearing Consumer Class

6.1 The Spending Multiplier at Macro Scale

The technology working class has reproduced the factory town demand dynamic at national and global scale. With a median salary of approximately $104,556 — more than double the U.S. median — the technology worker sits precisely at the floor of upper-middle-class income. Per Pew Research, upper-middle-class household income ranges from roughly $106,000 to $149,000.

The spending multiplier for high-income professional workers is estimated at 1.5× to 2×. At the scale of twelve million workers, this is not a marginal contribution — it is a structural pillar of the consumer economy. The entire cost structure of American professional life — housing, healthcare, childcare, private education, premium goods and services — has been calibrated over two decades to technology-worker incomes.

6.2 The Seattle Canary

Seattle is functioning as the concentrated, visible preview of what will play out across a hundred cities in diluted form. As highly paid tech employees depart, so do their paychecks. 450 restaurants — 16% of the city's total — closed in the first half of 2025. Commercial real estate vacancies are rising, new construction has halted, and Seattle faces a $146 million revenue shortfall from reduced sales and payroll tax collections. This is the demand desert dynamic — the same mechanism that hollowed out Lowell after the textile contraction — playing out in a modern city.

6.3 National Indicators

The leading indicators are confirming the trajectory. Tech salaries increased just 1.6% in 2025 — the lowest in fifteen years. Deloitte projects real consumer spending slowing to 1.6% in 2026, down from 2.6% in 2025. The U.S. added just 181,000 jobs across all of 2025, compared to 1.4+ million the year prior. GDP growth in late 2025 was explicitly boosted by AI data center investment — capital expenditure employing far fewer people per dollar than the workforces it replaces.

§ VII

§ VIIPillar Five: Mutually Assured Economic Destruction

7.1 Henry Ford's Insight and Its Modern Abandonment

In 1914, Henry Ford doubled his assembly line workers' wages to $5 a day — not out of philanthropy, but because he understood a structural requirement of industrial capitalism: capital requires consumers, and consumers require income. He wanted his workers to afford the cars they built. The flywheel only works when it spins.

Capitalism at scale requires a large, well-compensated consuming class to sustain the demand that generates returns that justify capital investment that creates employment that sustains the consuming class. This is explicitly circular. Every intervention that extracts labor value without replacing it with wage income that returns to the consumer economy is burning the track the flywheel runs on.

7.2 The 1929 Proof of Concept

The Great Depression is the most documented case study of what happens when capital extraction outpaces wage maintenance at scale. The cascade is self-reinforcing in both directions: rising wages create rising demand and rising profits, and collapsing wages create collapsing demand and collapsing profits. The wealthy did not escape. The mechanisms of their wealth — demand for their goods, servicing of their loans, valuations of their assets — all depended on the purchasing power of the classes below them.

7.3 The Extraction Without Recycling Problem

What distinguishes the current displacement from prior transitions is that the historical resolution mechanism — capital reinvestment in new physical production that created new employment — has been broken. In 2025, over 62,000 tech jobs were eliminated across nearly 300 companies even as those companies reported strong financial results. Median total compensation for S&P 500 CEOs rose to $17.1 million — a 9.7% increase — while the CEO-to-worker pay ratio reached approximately 285-to-1. The capital extracted from eliminated labor is flowing into stock buybacks, executive compensation, and AI infrastructure that employs far fewer people per dollar.

Economic performance has been poorer under luxury capitalism than under the more egalitarian post-1945 model. With new ways of securing easy money and boosting private rates of return, too much economic activity has become associated with the extraction of existing wealth, with a declining level of private investment and a weakened ability to withstand social and economic shocks.

— Stewart Lansley, The Richer, The Poorer, on the structural failure of concentrated capital extraction

7.4 The Luxury Market Signal

The upper class's own medium-term economic interests are already registering the damage. Major luxury conglomerates including LVMH and Kering are reporting easing sales following a three-year surge. The aspirational upper-middle class that drives volume luxury spending — the $400 sunglasses, the business-class upgrade — is precisely the demographic being hollowed out. The ultra-wealthy insulate themselves from downturns. The commercial ecosystem built to serve the professional class does not.

§ VIII

§ VIIIPillar Six: The Process Boy Problem

8.1 The Dominant Cultural Narrative

Beginning in 2023, as mass layoffs were eliminating tens of thousands of software engineers, a parallel narrative emerged: displaced workers should pivot to prompt engineering — instructing AI systems through natural language, explicitly marketed with the selling point that no coding or deep technical knowledge was required. Anthropic advertised prompt engineering roles at salaries as high as $375,000 with no coding requirement. Universities built programs. Bootcamps monetized certification tracks. A McKinsey Global Survey found 7% of AI-adopting organizations had already hired prompt engineers, and the role was widely declared the defining career of 2024.

The selling point — no technical knowledge required — was presented as democratization. It was, in historical terms, deskilling.

8.2 The Historical Exact Parallel

The "process boy" emerged from the British Industrial Revolution as a direct consequence of the power loom's displacement of master weavers. Factory owners discovered they could replace one skilled worker with several unskilled operatives, each performing a single repetitive motion. The process boy specialized in exactly one task and was ignorant of the fundamental principles of the trade in which he was engaged.

The process boy role was not a lie in the simple sense — there were jobs, they paid wages. But they were terminal roles: endpoints, not waypoints. They did not compound into expertise. They did not develop into senior mastery. And critically, they actively atrophied the worker's existing knowledge through disuse, ensuring the skilled craftsman who accepted the role became less capable of returning to skilled work than when he started.

Dimension 1820 British Factory 2023 Tech Industry
The Pitch "The loom is the future, adapt" "AI is the future, adapt"
The Role Master weaver → machine minder Software engineer → prompt engineer

8.3 The Collapse of the Role

The prompt engineering narrative did not require decades to refute — it required approximately eighteen months. By 2025, Microsoft surveys of 31,000 workers across 31 countries found prompt engineer ranked second to last among roles companies were considering hiring. Job postings peaked on Indeed in April 2023 and have been declining since.

AI is already eating its own. Prompt engineering has become something embedded in almost every role. It has turned from a job into a task very, very quickly.

— Malcolm Frank, CEO of TalentGenius, 2025

Sam Altman predicted this outcome in 2022: "I don't think we'll be doing prompt engineering in five years — you will just interface in language and get the computer to do whatever you want." He was correct. He was also, simultaneously, permitting his company to advertise $375,000 prompt engineering roles to workers who needed somewhere to go. The right hand knew what the left hand was building.

8.4 The Triple Harm

The prompt engineering misdirection caused harm in three compounding registers that the process boy dynamic alone did not produce. First, compression: the process boy era lasted decades; the prompt engineering era lasted eighteen months, giving workers no adaptive window. Second, active institutional misdirection: tech executives explicitly and loudly declared prompt engineering a career, built educational infrastructure around it, then deprecated the role before the first credentialed cohort could establish themselves. Third, the credentialing trap: workers paid tuition for bootcamp certifications in a skill that was obsolete before the ink dried. The process boy at least did not go into debt to learn to pull a lever.

§ IX

§ IXPillar Seven: Breaking the Pipeline — The Expert Famine

9.1 The Three-Stage Knowledge Transmission Pipeline

Every complex technical field in human history has depended on a three-stage knowledge transmission pipeline: formal education establishes foundational principles; apprenticeship and entry-level practice converts abstract knowledge into applied competence; expert practice produces senior practitioners who push the field forward and train the next generation. The pipeline is irreplaceable in the specific sense that its outputs — senior experts — cannot be produced by any other means. They must be grown from students through apprentices. Break any link in this chain and the pipeline stops producing experts within a single generation.

9.2 The British Catastrophe: A Documented Warning

Britain's failure to protect its skilled engineering pipeline during the Industrial Revolution is the most thoroughly documented cautionary case in the history of technological displacement. New industrial technologies rendered the apprenticeship system obsolete, encouraged the exploitation of low-skilled process labor, and produced a workforce ignorant of the fundamental principles of their trades.

The crafts which once supplied the comforts and even the luxuries of life have been so divided and subdivided that few masters of an art could now find the occasion to practice it; the system of apprenticeship, which had theretofore provided a succession of skilled workmen, became impracticable.

— Philadelphia engineer, 1870s, on the aftermath of industrial deskilling (Massachusetts Bureau of Statistics of Labor, 1907)

Germany and Sweden, which maintained and expanded their technical education systems during the same period, emerged as dominant industrial powers in precisely the industries that Britain had helped invent. Britain spent the following generation rebuilding the technical pipeline it had allowed to collapse.

9.3 The Modern Pipeline Is Fracturing Now

The pipeline fracture is not a future risk — it is a present, measurable reality. At the entry level: Indian IT services companies have reduced junior roles by 20–25% due to automation. Job platforms registered a 35% decline in junior tech positions across major EU countries in 2024. CS graduates face 6.1% unemployment in 2025 — nearly double the rate of philosophy majors — ranking 7th highest in unemployment among all college majors, despite CS degrees more than doubling in annual output from 52,000 to nearly 113,000 bachelor's degrees between 2013 and 2023.

At the education stage: enrollment in computer science programs dropped 8.1% in the 2025–2026 school year — the steepest decline of any field of study. More than 62% of 130 universities surveyed by the Computing Research Association reported decreased CS enrollment, with leaders explicitly citing AI-driven job displacement concerns. For the first time since the dot-com crash, UC campus CS enrollment dropped — falling 6% after a 3% decrease in 2024 — even as overall national college enrollment climbed 2%.

9.4 The Rational Actor Trap

Students are rational actors responding to market signals — and the signals are telling them to leave the pipeline. This rationality at the individual level constitutes irrationality at the systemic level: the classic collective action failure. The one enrollment exception is revealing: UC San Diego, which added a dedicated AI major, was the only UC campus to see CS enrollment grow. Students are fleeing the specific pipeline segment AI is eliminating and crowding into the operating layer of the displacing machine — the process boy dynamic repeating in enrollment data in real time.

9.5 The Expert Famine

AI systems can currently handle routine code generation, pattern-matched debugging, and templated documentation. They cannot handle system architecture at genuine scale, security threat modeling against novel attack vectors, the deep institutional knowledge of how complex legacy systems behave under stress, or the creative synthesis required to develop genuinely new technical paradigms. These capabilities live in senior engineers.

Senior engineers were once junior engineers, who were once computer science students. The pipeline requires approximately ten to fifteen years from enrollment to genuine senior capability. If the student and junior layers are collapsed now, the result will be a severe and structurally irreversible shortage of senior-capability engineers arriving in approximately 2034 to 2038 — precisely as AI systems become complex enough to require deep human expertise for oversight, error correction, and advancement. The civilization automating away the pipeline for producing that expertise is dismantling the cognitive infrastructure its own technological future depends upon.

§ X

§ XSynthesis: The Sum of the Arguments

The seven pillars developed in this paper converge on a single unified conclusion that can be stated plainly, without qualification:

The artificial intelligence-driven displacement of the technology working class is not a normal economic correction. It is a structural transformation that simultaneously destroys the first post-geographic working class in the history of industrial capitalism; removes the load-bearing consumer class from a service economy calibrated over two decades to their spending; extracts the resulting capital value upward without recycling it into new labor demand; inflicts mutually assured medium-term economic damage on the ownership class through the consumption collapse it creates; misdirects displaced workers into terminal deskilling roles dressed as adaptive opportunity; and breaks the education-to-apprenticeship-to-expert knowledge transmission pipeline that will be required to oversee, correct, and advance the very AI systems currently doing the displacing.

The historical precedent is unambiguous. Britain's failure to protect its technical pipeline during the Industrial Revolution produced a competitive catastrophe that took a generation to begin reversing, in a national economy with no international competitors operating at comparable technological scale. The current displacement is occurring globally and simultaneously, with no equivalent period of grace for recovery.

The Industrial Revolution's resolution took approximately 80 years, several political revolutions, two world wars, and the construction of the 20th century welfare state — not primarily from altruism, but because the ownership class eventually recognized that the alternative was a demand collapse severe enough to destroy the wealth-generating system itself. Henry Ford understood this in 1914. The New Deal understood it in 1933. The question being tested in the present moment is whether the ownership class and its political representatives will understand it before or after the flywheel seizes.

The velocity of the current transition provides reason for alarm rather than comfort. The Industrial Revolution's displacement took 60 years to fully manifest. The AI displacement is compressing a comparable structural transformation into a decade. The political and institutional capacity to respond has not accelerated to match the pace of the disruption, and the cultural apparatus is actively directing workers toward the process boy solution rather than toward the institutional demands that historical evidence suggests are necessary.

The prompt engineer is the process boy. The demand desert is spreading from Seattle outward. The pipeline is fracturing at the enrollment stage. The flywheel is losing momentum.

The sum of the arguments is this: we are not watching a painful but ultimately productive creative destruction. We are watching the systematic dismantlement of the economic, institutional, and cognitive infrastructure that industrial capitalism requires to function — conducted in the name of efficiency, at a speed that forecloses adaptive response, guided by a cultural narrative that has been refuted by history before and will be refuted again, at greater cost, if the present trajectory continues uncorrected.
